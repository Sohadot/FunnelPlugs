Plug class prioritization

Order interventions by structural dependency, not by ease of installation. The sequence is governed — not chosen.

Scope

This concept defines the governed logic for sequencing structural interventions when a diagnostic reveals multiple missing plug classes. Plug class prioritization is the decision framework that connects diagnostic output to an ordered intervention plan — governed by structural dependency relationships, propagation effects, and revenue impact sequencing under the Funnelplugs Protocol.

What plug class prioritization is

Plug class prioritization is the governed logic for sequencing structural interventions when a diagnostic reveals multiple missing plug classes in a commercial path. This is not an arbitrary choice between available interventions. It is not a budget constraint exercise that installs the cheapest plug first or the quickest plug first. It is a structural decision — governed by dependency relationships between plug classes, propagation effects that spread structural failure across the path, and revenue impact sequencing that determines where the highest-cost structural absence sits.

The need for prioritization arises from the diagnostic reality that structurally incomplete paths typically exhibit multiple failure classes simultaneously. A path diagnosed with Trust Collapse, Friction Overload, and Recovery Absence needs three distinct intervention categories. The question is not whether to address all three — all three must be addressed for full structural integrity. The question is the order: which structural absence, left unaddressed, prevents the other interventions from producing their intended outcome?

Prioritization is consequential because out-of-sequence intervention wastes resources. Installing a Recovery Systems Layer before Trust Infrastructure is in place generates re-engagement sequences that reach buyers who remain unequipped to convert — the recovery effort is executing on a path that still fails at the trust layer. Installing conversion optimization before entry-level alignment is established means optimizing the conversion experience for buyers who arrive with mismatched expectations. In both cases, the intervention resource is consumed and the structural revenue problem persists. Prioritization logic exists to prevent this.

The Plug Ontology classifies the plug classes. The leak-plug mapping defines the correspondence between failure classes and intervention classes. Plug class prioritization governs the sequencing of those interventions once the map has been applied and multiple plug classes have been identified as required.

The dependency principle

The dependency principle is the foundational rule of plug class prioritization: upstream plugs must be installed before downstream plugs because downstream plugs depend on upstream structural conditions that upstream plugs create. Violating this principle does not produce a suboptimal result — it produces a non-result. The downstream plug installs correctly and then operates on structural conditions that make it ineffective.

The most common dependency violation is installing a Recovery Plug before establishing Trust Infrastructure. Recovery systems are designed to re-engage buyers who exited before completing the intended action. When those buyers exited because Trust Infrastructure was absent — because the offer lacked credibility, because social proof was missing, because risk reversal was inadequate — the recovery sequence re-engages them into the same trust-deficient environment. They are contacted, they return, they encounter the same structural absence, and they exit again. The recovery effort has consumed resources and produced no structural improvement.

The second common dependency violation is optimizing conversion-stage elements before entry-stage alignment is established. If Message-Path Misalignment is present — if arriving buyers carry expectations that the path architecture does not honor — then conversion optimization at the offer stage is applied to buyers who are already structurally disqualified by misalignment. The conversion elements are correctly specified but receiving misaligned traffic. Entry-level structural restoration must precede offer-stage optimization because the offer stage depends on receiving buyers who entered with the correct expectations.

The dependency principle is not a recommendation — it is a structural constraint. Plug installation that violates it produces investment without structural return. The principle governs every prioritization decision in the sequence framework.

The four prioritization criteria

When structural dependencies do not fully resolve the sequencing question — when two or more plug classes have no direct dependency relationship with each other — four prioritization criteria govern the ordering decision.

Criterion 1 — Entry dependency. Does this plug class enable buyer entry into the path? Entry-enabling plugs take absolute priority because their absence means that no subsequent structural element operates on qualified traffic. If Alignment Reinforcement is absent, misaligned traffic enters the path and all downstream conversion infrastructure operates on visitors who were never structurally equipped to convert. Entry dependency is a binary criterion: either the plug governs path entry or it does not. Those that do take precedence over all non-entry-governing plugs regardless of the other criteria.

Criterion 2 — Propagation risk. Does this structural absence corrupt adjacent stages even in the absence of a direct installation dependency? Some plug class absences produce downstream degradation without technically creating a dependency relationship. Trust Integrity weakness, for example, may not prevent buyers from reaching the offer stage, but its absence actively suppresses offer acceptance — corrupting the performance of the entire conversion layer. High propagation risk plugs take priority over low-propagation-risk plugs when entry dependency status is equal.

Criterion 3 — Revenue impact. What is the estimated per-session revenue cost of this structural absence, derived from the behavioral signal data collected by the diagnostic signal layers? Revenue impact scoring provides a quantitative basis for prioritization when dependency and propagation criteria do not fully differentiate between competing plug candidates. The plug class whose absence costs more revenue per session, weighted by its position in the conversion path, takes priority when other criteria are equivalent.

Criterion 4 — Installation complexity. What is the resource requirement relative to the structural impact of this plug class? When two plug classes have equivalent entry dependency status, equivalent propagation risk, and equivalent revenue impact, installation complexity becomes the tiebreaker: the lower-complexity installation proceeds first because it delivers structural improvement more quickly, validating the diagnostic and improving path performance while the higher-complexity plug is being prepared. Complexity is always the last criterion — it is never applied as a primary reason to delay a high-dependency, high-propagation, high-impact plug installation.

Priority sequencing framework

Applying the dependency principle and four criteria produces a governed priority sequence applicable to any multi-plug diagnostic output. The sequence reflects structural dependencies, not an arbitrary ranking of plug class types.

First priority: Entry-level plugs. Alignment Reinforcement, Audience Qualification, and related entry-governing plug classes are installed first. These plugs determine whether the path receives buyers who are structurally positioned to move through it. If entry-level plugs are absent, no downstream investment produces its designed return — all subsequent plug installations are receiving misqualified traffic. Entry-level plugs create the structural conditions on which every downstream plug depends.

Second priority: Trust-level plugs. Trust Infrastructure, Authority Signaling, and related trust-governing plug classes are installed after entry is stabilized. Trust-level plugs must be in place before conversion-level plug optimization is meaningful because conversion mechanisms operate on the basis of trust that trust infrastructure creates. Buyers arrive correctly qualified (entry restored), encounter the trust architecture (trust installed), and are then equipped to respond to the conversion elements.

Third priority: Conversion-level plugs. Decision Stage Integrity, Offer Architecture, and related conversion-governing plug classes are installed after trust-level structure is in place. At this point, the path has qualified entry and trust infrastructure — conversion-level optimization is now applied to buyers who are positioned to act on it. Friction Reduction Layer installation belongs here as well — it operates on buyers who are present and motivated, removing the last structural resistance to the intended action.

Fourth priority: Recovery-level plugs. Recovery Systems Layer, Re-engagement Architecture, and related recovery plug classes are installed after the primary path achieves structural integrity. Recovery plugs capture revenue from buyers who exit despite a complete primary path — exits that represent delayed rather than lost conversions. Installing recovery infrastructure on a primary path that is still structurally incomplete creates recovery sequences that re-engage buyers into an unresolved failure — wasting recovery resources and producing false diagnostic signals.

Continuous: Intelligence-level plugs. Measurement Architecture, Diagnostic Signal Layers, and the analytical infrastructure that enables ongoing classification are not sequenced within the above priorities — they are installed and maintained across all stages. The intelligence layer must be operational at every stage of the sequence because it produces the signal data that validates each plug installation, identifies emerging failure classes, and governs protocol compliance.

The engine applies this logic

The Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine applies the prioritization logic as a structured output layer after failure class identification and leak-plug mapping are complete. The engine accepts the diagnostic inputs — behavioral signal data, declared path conditions, dimensional weakness profiles — and produces a prioritized plug installation sequence governed by the dependency principle and the four criteria defined in this document.

The prioritization output is not a recommendation list. It is a governed structural sequence with the dependency reasoning made explicit: each plug class in the sequence carries the justification for its position — which dependency it resolves, what propagation risk it eliminates, what revenue impact governs its ranking. This transparency is what makes the sequence auditable and the intervention plan defensible.

The Plug Ontology provides the classification vocabulary. The Protocol governs the diagnostic workflow. The Plug Registry provides the installation specifications for each prioritized plug class. Together, these elements ensure that prioritization produces not just a sequence but a complete, governed intervention plan with classification, mapping, sequencing, and specification — from diagnosis to structural restoration.
