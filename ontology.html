The Plug Ontology

Revenue does not disappear. It escapes through unnamed structural gaps. This ontology names those gaps, classifies their structural logic, and establishes the governed vocabulary for diagnosing and correcting missing intervention layers across commercial paths.

Ontology purpose

The Plug Ontology v1.0 is the classification layer the entire Funnelplugs system operates from. It defines ten structural failure classes—the underlying conditions that cause commercial paths to leak revenue, lose buyers, or fail to recover what they cannot close. The Protocol uses these classes for Classification. The Standard defines the integrity conditions each class tests against. The Registry organizes interventions by failure class. The Engine maps declared funnel conditions against this ontology to produce diagnostic output.

Opening doctrine

Most failure in commercial paths is diagnosed at the symptom level. A team observes a drop in conversion rate, an increase in cart abandonment, or a decline in lead quality and responds with the tools available: a new call to action, a revised headline, a different ad variation. The symptom shifts slightly. The structural condition persists.

The reason this cycle continues is not incompetence. It is the absence of a governed vocabulary for naming structural failure. When the failure mode has no name, it has no address. When it has no address, it cannot be prioritized, owned, or systematically corrected.

Funnels do not fail because one isolated step is weak. They fail because the commercial path contains missing intervention layers that no team owns, no metric fully explains, and no single tool fully governs. The Plug Ontology exists to name those layers.

What is a plug?

A plug is a structural intervention layer. It is not a tactic. It is not a feature. It is the class of structural mechanism required to address a specific category of failure in a commercial path.

The distinction matters. A tactic responds to a signal. A plug addresses a structural condition. Installing a social proof widget in response to low conversion is a tactic. Diagnosing that Trust Infrastructure Failure is the dominant structural condition at the commitment stage, then systematically building the legitimacy layer that stage requires, is installing a plug.

Plugs are defined by the failure class they correct, not by the specific implementation used to correct it. Different commercial paths may require different implementations of the same plug class. The ontology governs the class. The operator governs the implementation.

What is a missing plug?

A missing plug is the structural absence of an intervention layer that the path requires in order to carry buyers from intent to completion without structural collapse.

Missing plugs are not visible the way broken elements are visible. A broken button is visible. A missing plug is visible only through its effects: buyers who exit where no obvious barrier exists, paths that function technically but fail commercially, conversion rates that do not respond to tactical adjustment because the structural condition producing them has never been addressed.

The defining characteristic of a missing plug is that its absence is structural, not situational. It is not a temporary oversight. It is a systematic gap in the architecture of the commercial path—one that will continue producing the same failure pattern until the plug is identified, installed, and maintained.

Why commercial paths leak

Commercial paths leak for one of three structural reasons.

First, required intervention layers were never built. The path was constructed to move buyers from one stage to the next without accounting for the structural conditions that make each transition possible. Trust infrastructure was not installed because trust problems were not anticipated. Recovery systems were not built because abandonment was not modeled as a recoverable event. The missing plug was never placed because its necessity was never named.

Second, intervention layers existed but were not maintained. The path was sound at one point in its lifecycle. Then a team change occurred, a tracking event stopped firing, a recovery sequence stopped sending, an offer page fell out of alignment with the product it was promoting. The plug was present. Then it degraded. The failure mode it was correcting returned.

Third, intervention layers are present but misaligned. Social proof exists but is placed at the wrong stage. Recovery sequences exist but re-enter the path at the beginning rather than at the abandonment stage. Decision guidance exists but addresses the wrong decision. The structural response is present but applied incorrectly, creating the appearance of completeness while the functional gap remains.

All three conditions produce the same result: revenue escapes through a structural gap that has no name and therefore no owner. This ontology assigns those gaps their names.

The ten ontology classes

Plug Ontology v1.0 — governed classification of structural commercial failure

  • Class 01 — Intent Continuity Failure

    Definition. The commercial path fails to maintain alignment between the user’s declared or implied intent and the content, offer, or action they encounter at each subsequent stage. The path receives intent but does not carry it forward coherently. What the buyer arrived expecting and what the path delivers diverge before any conversion decision point is reached.

    Where it appears. At the entry layer of the commercial path—the transition from traffic source to landing surface, from advertisement to destination content, from search query to page response. Also appears at any internal stage transition where the path resets context without re-establishing intent alignment with the buyer’s current frame.

    What it damages. Entry conversion rate, traffic investment efficiency, and the buyer’s willingness to continue into deeper stages of the path. When intent is not met at entry, commitment to the path dissolves before any commercial decision is reached.

    Typical symptoms. High single-page exit rates from paid traffic. Low scroll depth on entry pages. Messaging that describes the brand from a product perspective when the buyer arrived with a problem perspective. Significant cost-per-click expenditure with poor downstream conversion and no identifiable technical failure.

    Required plug logic. Systematic mapping of intent signals across all traffic sources. Calibration of landing surfaces to match the specific intent state driving each visit. Stage-by-stage continuity review verifying each path transition carries forward the correct framing rather than resetting to a generic brand presentation. Plug class: Commercial Path Continuity.

    Related protocol stage. Detection—where path zones are mapped against the integrity standard to identify incomplete alignment.

    Related standard dimension. Flow Continuity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. The engine assesses intent continuity through commercial path questions. Declared signals of traffic-source misalignment and entry-stage drop-off route diagnostic output toward this class.

  • Class 02 — Trust Infrastructure Failure

    Definition. The commercial path lacks the required signals, proof layers, and legitimacy markers that a buyer needs at decision-critical points to proceed with confidence. The path presents an offer or requests an action without establishing the structural conditions necessary for trust to exist. The buyer is asked to commit before the path has earned the right to ask.

    Where it appears. Most acutely at conversion moments—checkout pages, order forms, subscription confirmation, high-commitment calls to action. Also present at any stage where the buyer is asked to transfer personal information, payment credentials, or significant commitment without adequate trust infrastructure in place. Applies across funnels, lead paths, checkout paths, and onboarding flows.

    What it damages. Conversion rate at final commitment stages, buyer confidence in the brand, and the perceived legitimacy of the offer. Trust Infrastructure Failure creates a structural ceiling on conversion that traffic optimization, copy revision, and offer adjustment cannot overcome, because the failure is structural, not presentational.

    Typical symptoms. High abandonment rate at checkout or order pages with no corresponding technical failure. Support queries questioning security or legitimacy. Low repeat conversion rates despite initial buyer engagement. Absence of visible social proof, security signals, or third-party validation at the decision stages where they are most needed.

    Required plug logic. Audit of trust signal presence and positioning at every decision-critical stage. Installation of legitimacy markers appropriate to the level of commitment being requested. Sequential trust construction across the full path so that the conversion moment is preceded by an adequate trust foundation rather than preceded only by persuasion. Plug class: Trust Infrastructure.

    Related protocol stage. Classification—identified failure is assigned to this class based on the stage and symptom pattern of the trust gap.

    Related standard dimension. Trust Integrity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Trust Infrastructure Failure is one of the primary diagnostic outputs of the Revenue Leak Engine. The engine’s trust-related question set maps declared buyer behavior and path conditions to the Trust Collapse leak class, which corresponds to this ontology class.

  • Class 03 — Decision Clarity Failure

    Definition. The commercial path presents an offer, option, or action request that the buyer cannot evaluate with sufficient confidence to make a committed decision. The cognitive cost of deciding exceeds the buyer’s readiness to act, producing hesitation, stalling, or exit. The path is asking for a decision before it has provided the structural conditions for one to be made.

    Where it appears. At offer presentation stages—pricing pages, plan comparison views, multi-option checkout flows, and any surface where the buyer must choose between alternatives or assess the terms of a single offer. Also present in content-heavy paths where information volume obscures the decision rather than clarifying it. Distinct from Trust Infrastructure Failure: trust may be present, but the offer itself is structurally illegible.

    What it damages. Decision momentum, offer comprehension, and the buyer’s ability to move from evaluation to commitment. Decision Clarity Failure is a structural problem, not a copy problem. Rewriting the headline does not resolve it. Restructuring how the offer is presented and how the decision is framed does.

    Typical symptoms. Extended session time on pricing or offer pages followed by high exit rates with no action taken. Support queries requesting clarification on offer terms, pricing logic, or feature differences that should be clear from the page. Multiple page revisits before abandonment. Buyers who understand the product but cannot evaluate the specific offer presented.

    Required plug logic. Offer architecture review to reduce cognitive friction at decision stages. Clear information hierarchy: the most decision-critical facts must be immediately visible and interpretable. Explicit differentiation where multiple paths exist. Removal of decision-irrelevant complexity from conversion surfaces. Plug class: Decision Guidance Layer.

    Related protocol stage. Classification—assigned based on the stage at which the clarity failure occurs and the symptom pattern it produces.

    Related standard dimension. Trust Integrity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Decision Clarity Failure maps to the Decision Ambiguity leak class in the engine’s diagnostic taxonomy. Declared signals of extended evaluation time, option confusion, and clarity-related support contact route output toward this class.

  • Class 04 — Flow Continuity Failure

    Definition. The commercial path contains structural interruptions, competing navigation options, or momentum breaks that allow the buyer’s forward progress to stall or terminate before reaching the conversion event. The path does not hold the buyer in motion between stages. Movement from intent to completion is available but not structurally supported.

    Where it appears. Throughout multi-step paths—checkout sequences, application flows, onboarding journeys, lead nurture paths. Particularly present at stage transitions where the buyer must initiate the next step without structural guidance, or where external navigation options compete with the intended forward direction.

    What it damages. Path completion rate, step-to-step conversion, and the compounding investment made in bringing the buyer to each stage. Flow Continuity Failure wastes all upstream effort by allowing exits at structurally preventable moments.

    Typical symptoms. High drop-off rates at specific identifiable steps with no corresponding technical failure. Buyers reaching the middle of a multi-step flow without completing it. Checkout abandonment concentrated at one or two consistent internal steps. Exit to homepage or navigation from within an active conversion sequence. Form abandonment on non-required fields or at stage boundaries.

    Required plug logic. Stage-by-stage flow audit mapping every structural exit opportunity within conversion sequences. Removal or suppression of competing navigation options inside conversion-critical paths. Progress indicators where multi-step completion is required. Structural micro-commitment design to maintain forward momentum. Plug class: Flow Continuity Layer.

    Related protocol stage. Detection and Classification—path mapping identifies the specific interruption points; classification assigns the failure to this class.

    Related standard dimension. Flow Continuity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Flow Continuity Failure maps to the Flow Break leak class in the engine’s diagnostic taxonomy. Declared step-drop signals, navigation exit patterns, and sequential abandonment data route diagnostic output toward this class.

  • Class 05 — Commitment Boundary Failure

    Definition. The commercial path does not adequately define or reduce the perceived risk, reversibility, or commitment magnitude of the final conversion action. The buyer reaches the commitment moment unable to accurately assess what they are agreeing to, or insufficiently prepared for the level of commitment being requested. The path closes the commercial case without closing the commitment case.

    Where it appears. At the final stage of the conversion path—order confirmation, subscription initiation, contract submission, account creation with payment. Distinct from Decision Clarity Failure, which occurs during offer evaluation; Commitment Boundary Failure occurs at the moment of execution, when the offer is understood but the act of committing carries unresolved structural ambiguity.

    What it damages. Final-step conversion rate and the psychological readiness of the buyer to execute. Also damages post-conversion satisfaction and refund or cancellation rates, as buyers who proceed without boundary clarity often reverse the commitment shortly after.

    Typical symptoms. Last-step abandonment despite high engagement throughout the rest of the path. Elevated refund or cancellation rates within a short post-conversion window. Buyers who complete a form but do not submit. Support queries immediately following conversion asking for clarification on terms or what happens next.

    Required plug logic. Explicit commitment framing at the final action stage: what the buyer is agreeing to, what happens immediately after conversion, and what options exist post-commitment. Risk reduction signals positioned immediately adjacent to the commitment action element. Clear terms presentation without burying commitment-relevant information in secondary locations. Plug class: Friction Reduction Layer.

    Related protocol stage. Classification and Intervention—failure is classified by its final-stage location and symptom pattern, then addressed with commitment-boundary-specific plug logic.

    Related standard dimension. Trust Integrity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Commitment Boundary Failure contributes to the Friction Overload leak class in the engine’s diagnostic taxonomy, specifically at the commitment execution stage. Final-step abandonment signals and post-conversion reversal patterns route output toward this class.

  • Class 06 — Recovery Absence Failure

    Definition. The commercial path lacks the structural mechanisms required to re-engage buyers who exited before conversion. When abandonment occurs, the system has no governed response. The path treats every exit as final, eliminating a recoverable portion of commercial value that the active path could not capture but a recovery system could.

    Where it appears. As a post-path layer—the structural absence is not visible within the active funnel but becomes decisive when abandonment events occur. Applies to cart abandonment, form abandonment, lead drop-off, trial expiry without conversion, and any non-completion event where re-engagement within a defined window is commercially viable.

    What it damages. Revenue recovery rate, total path yield, and the efficiency of the entire acquisition investment. Recovery Absence Failure means the commercial system accepts a lower ceiling on conversion without attempting to reclaim the portion the active path could not close. Every unrecovered abandonment represents compounded cost.

    Typical symptoms. No re-engagement sequence following cart or form abandonment. No retargeting logic tied to specific abandonment stages. Customer records showing high evaluation engagement followed by permanent, unaddressed exit. No differentiated recovery path for users who reached different stages before abandonment.

    Required plug logic. Abandonment detection at each stage where recovery logic is commercially viable. Sequenced re-engagement designed to address the specific stage of abandonment rather than generic follow-up divorced from context. Timing logic calibrated to buyer decision cycles. Recovery paths that re-enter the commercial journey at the correct abandonment stage, not at the beginning. Plug class: Abandonment Recovery Logic.

    Related protocol stage. Detection—abandonment rate and recovery infrastructure absence are identified in the path mapping stage.

    Related standard dimension. Recovery Readiness.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Recovery Absence Failure maps directly to the Recovery Absence leak class in the engine’s diagnostic taxonomy. Declared absence of recovery sequences and abandonment-stage data route diagnostic output toward this class.

  • Class 07 — Proof Alignment Failure

    Definition. The commercial path contains social proof, testimonials, case evidence, or validation signals that are present but structurally misaligned—positioned at the wrong stage, directed at the wrong buyer profile, or addressing the wrong objection for the decision moment where they appear. The proof exists. It does not function because it is not structurally connected to the hesitation it should resolve.

    Where it appears. Throughout the path wherever proof signals are deployed. Most consequential when misalignment occurs at high-commitment stages. The presence of proof does not produce the expected conversion support because the proof speaks to a different buyer state or a different concern than is active at the stage where it appears.

    What it damages. The persuasive efficiency of the proof layer, buyer objection resolution capacity, and the rate at which buyers move through hesitation stages. Proof Alignment Failure is particularly difficult to diagnose because teams confirm that social proof is present and conclude it is functioning, without examining whether it is positioned to address the active objection at each specific stage.

    Typical symptoms. Testimonials present at checkout that address product quality rather than purchase safety. Case studies positioned early where intent has not yet been established. Proof content that reflects a different buyer segment than the one currently in the path. Social proof that improves engagement metrics but does not improve conversion metrics at the stages it occupies.

    Required plug logic. Proof audit mapping existing validation assets against the specific hesitations active at each stage. Repositioning of proof assets to create stage-specific alignment between buyer objection and available validation. Acquisition of proof content targeting under-served decision stages where current assets do not address the dominant objection. Plug class: Alignment Reinforcement Layer.

    Related protocol stage. Classification and Intervention—the failure is classified by the misalignment pattern; intervention redesigns proof placement around stage-specific hesitation logic.

    Related standard dimension. Trust Integrity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Proof Alignment Failure contributes to the Trust Collapse leak class in the engine’s diagnostic taxonomy, specifically in the proof-presence-without-proof-function signal pattern.

  • Class 08 — Offer-Action Mismatch

    Definition. The commercial path presents an offer in one context and requests a conversion action in a different context, without adequate structural bridging between them. The mental model the buyer formed during the offer encounter does not match the action they are asked to take, producing disorientation, hesitation, or exit. The offer promised one thing; the path asks for another.

    Where it appears. Most clearly at the transition between marketing content and conversion surface—from advertisement to landing page, from email offer to checkout, from content recommendation to product page. Also appears within a single surface when the call to action does not accurately represent the destination or commitment level it initiates.

    What it damages. Click-through coherence, landing page conversion rate, and buyer trust in the consistency of the path. Offer-Action Mismatch produces a specific disorientation: the buyer trusted the offer they encountered, but the action presented feels like a different agreement. The trust earned by the offer is not transferred to the action.

    Typical symptoms. High click-through rate with low landing page conversion rate, without a technical explanation for the gap. Buyers who engage strongly with content but do not respond to calls to action at expected rates. Conversion rates that vary significantly across traffic sources in ways that correlate with how the offer is framed in each source, not with audience quality differences.

    Required plug logic. Message continuity audit across every offer-to-action transition in the path. Calibration of call-to-action language to match the offer framing that preceded it. Landing surface review confirming the first experience after an action matches the expectation established by the offer. Correction of any gap between what is promised in the offer context and what is delivered in the action context. Plug class: Alignment Reinforcement Layer.

    Related protocol stage. Detection—path mapping identifies the specific transition where offer and action diverge.

    Related standard dimension. Flow Continuity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Offer-Action Mismatch maps to the Message-Path Misalignment leak class in the engine’s diagnostic taxonomy. Declared signals of traffic-source-specific conversion variation and post-click abandonment patterns route diagnostic output toward this class.

  • Class 09 — Measurement Blindness Failure

    Definition. The commercial path lacks the signal infrastructure required to identify where structural failure is occurring, which failure class is active, and what the operational magnitude of each gap is. Without measurement, the path operates without diagnostic capacity. Failure exists but cannot be located, classified, or addressed with structural precision.

    Where it appears. As a systemic layer across the entire path rather than at a specific stage. Measurement Blindness Failure does not produce failure directly; it prevents existing failure from being detected, located, and addressed. It is the structural condition that makes all other failure classes invisible and therefore persistent.

    What it damages. The organization’s capacity to diagnose, prioritize, and correct structural failure. Also damages resource allocation efficiency—teams without adequate measurement invest intervention effort in the wrong stages, addressing visible signals while structural causes remain invisible. It compounds every other failure class by extending the period during which those failures are unnamed and unaddressed.

    Typical symptoms. Conversion event tracking absent or unverified at commercially significant stages. Stage-level drop-off rates unknown or available only as aggregate totals without stage-specific breakdown. Session analysis and behavioral data absent from conversion-critical surfaces. Attribution logic missing from paid traffic paths. Reliance on top-level metrics that mask stage-level structural failure.

    Required plug logic. Verified event tracking at every stage where structural failure would be commercially significant if present. Funnel visualization configured to reveal stage-level drop-off rates rather than only entry and exit totals. Session analysis deployment on conversion-critical surfaces. Attribution logic connecting traffic source to stage-level conversion outcomes with sufficient granularity to support structural diagnosis. Plug class: Diagnostic Signal Layers.

    Related protocol stage. Detection—the protocol cannot complete its path mapping function without the signal layer this class addresses.

    Related standard dimension. Flow Continuity.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. Measurement Blindness Failure maps to the Diagnostic Signal Layers concept in the engine’s reference taxonomy. Declared absence of event tracking, funnel visualization, or behavioral analysis data routes the engine to flag this class as a prerequisite failure before other diagnostic output can be treated as reliable.

  • Class 10 — System Ownership Failure

    Definition. The commercial path lacks defined ownership, maintenance governance, or systematic integrity monitoring for its critical components. Path elements operate without a responsible party, a review cycle, or a failure alert. The path degrades over time because no structural accountability mechanism exists to prevent or detect that degradation.

    Where it appears. At the governance layer across the entire commercial path. System Ownership Failure is not localized to a single stage; it manifests as progressive structural decay when no governance mechanism exists to maintain path integrity over time. Every other failure class that goes undetected and uncorrected for extended periods is, in part, a consequence of System Ownership Failure.

    What it damages. Long-term path integrity, operational continuity, and the ability to compound improvements over time. Without system ownership, all structural corrections are temporary. Pages go dead. Tracking events stop firing. Recovery sequences cease sending. Offers become misaligned with current products. The path accumulates failure in proportion to the absence of governance.

    Typical symptoms. Broken internal links within conversion paths discovered through routine audit rather than alert. Tracking events configured but no longer firing at commercially significant stages. Offer pages that no longer reflect current pricing, availability, or product configuration. Recovery sequences referencing discontinued products. Conversion paths that performed well historically but have silently degraded. No systematic alert for critical path failures.

    Required plug logic. Ownership assignment for each critical path component with a defined review frequency and accountability structure. Monitoring infrastructure for conversion events, page availability, and tracking integrity with alert logic. Documented path architecture that reflects the live system. Change governance protocol requiring integrity review before modifications to conversion-critical components are deployed. Plug class: Dimensional Weakness Mapping.

    Related protocol stage. All stages—System Ownership Failure is the systemic condition that allows failures identified by every protocol stage to persist and recur.

    Related standard dimension. All standard dimensions. System Ownership Failure is the governance condition that allows every other integrity dimension to degrade when not actively maintained.

    Related diagnostic engine relevance. System Ownership Failure maps to the Dimensional Weakness Mapping concept in the engine’s reference taxonomy. Declared patterns of recurring failure, historical path degradation, and structural inconsistencies without identified owners route diagnostic output toward this class as a systemic compounding condition.

How the ontology relates to the FunnelPlugs Protocol

The FunnelPlugs Protocol operates in three stages: Detection, Classification, and Intervention. The Plug Ontology governs the Classification stage.

When Detection identifies a zone of the commercial path that is structurally incomplete, Classification assigns the failure to a class defined in this ontology. This is not a loose categorization. It is a precise structural assignment that determines which intervention logic applies, which standard dimension has been violated, and which diagnostic engine output is relevant.

Without the ontology, Classification would produce informal descriptions—“the checkout feels untrustworthy,” “the flow is breaking somewhere,” “the offer is confusing.” These descriptions may be accurate. They are not actionable with structural precision. The ontology converts informal observations into governed structural addresses: Trust Infrastructure Failure at the commitment stage. Decision Clarity Failure at the pricing surface. Recovery Absence Failure across the entire post-abandonment layer.

Each class in this ontology is, in structural terms, a valid output of the Protocol’s Classification stage. The Protocol is the process. The ontology provides the vocabulary that gives that process its precision.

How the ontology relates to the FunnelPlugs Standard

The Funnel Integrity Standard defines the positive structural conditions that a sound commercial path must satisfy. Each standard dimension—Trust Integrity, Flow Continuity, Recovery Readiness—represents an integrity condition the path must uphold.

The Plug Ontology and the Standard are structurally complementary. Each failure class in this ontology maps to one or more standard dimensions. A path that satisfies the Trust Integrity dimension does not exhibit Trust Infrastructure Failure, Decision Clarity Failure, Commitment Boundary Failure, or Proof Alignment Failure. A path that satisfies the Flow Continuity dimension does not exhibit Intent Continuity Failure, Flow Continuity Failure, or Offer-Action Mismatch.

The Standard defines what integrity looks like. The Ontology defines what its absence looks like. A path measured against the Standard, and found wanting, can have that failure classified using the Ontology. A path diagnosed using the Ontology can have every failure class found mapped back to a specific standard dimension that is not being satisfied.

The Standard and the Ontology together provide both the positive specification and the negative classification vocabulary required for complete structural governance of a commercial path.

How the ontology powers the Diagnostic Engine

The Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine generates governed diagnostic output by mapping declared funnel conditions against the Plug Ontology. The engine does not produce generic optimization recommendations. It produces a structural failure classification: a determination of which ontology class represents the dominant failure mode in the conditions the user has declared.

The engine’s question set is designed to surface the signal patterns that differentiate one ontology class from another. Trust Infrastructure Failure and Decision Clarity Failure both produce hesitation at conversion stages, but they produce different observable patterns and require different interventions. The engine’s diagnostic logic uses declared signals to distinguish between them and assign a primary class.

The engine’s output—the primary leak class, the integrity band, and the required intervention priority—is an operationalized application of this ontology. When the engine identifies Trust Collapse as the dominant failure mode, it is identifying structural conditions most consistent with Trust Infrastructure Failure or Proof Alignment Failure as classified here. When it identifies Decision Ambiguity, it is identifying structural conditions consistent with Decision Clarity Failure.

The ontology is the intellectual foundation that makes the engine’s output meaningful rather than procedural. The engine is the operational tool that makes the ontology applicable to a live commercial path without requiring manual classification expertise at every diagnosis.

Governance note

The Plug Ontology v1.0 is a governed document. It will not be modified to accommodate specific tool categories, vendor preferences, or short-term commercial considerations. Its classes are defined by structural logic, not by the availability of solutions that address those classes.

The ten classes in this document represent the set of structural failure modes that produce revenue leakage across commercial paths as understood at the time of this version. They are not exhaustive of every possible failure scenario, but they are sufficient to classify the structural conditions that produce the most common and consequential patterns of commercial path failure.

Future versions of this ontology will extend or refine the classification system as structural evidence warrants. Changes to class definitions, boundaries, or scope will be documented with versioning. No changes will be made to this document without version increment and documented rationale.

This document is maintained as part of the Funnelplugs sovereign reference layer. Its function is to provide a stable, defensible, and institutionally credible classification vocabulary for structural funnel failure. That function requires stability. Stability requires governance.
