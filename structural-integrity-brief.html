Structural Integrity Brief

A condensed reference for structural assessment of commercial digital paths: the three formal integrity dimensions, ten leak classes, diagnostic criteria, and self-assessment framework.

Brief scope

This brief summarizes the core architecture of the Funnelplugs structural integrity system. It is designed for practitioners who need a rapid orientation to the classification framework, strategists conducting initial path assessments, and acquirers evaluating the intellectual infrastructure of this asset. For the complete system, follow the references throughout.

What structural integrity means

Structural integrity is the property of a commercial path in which all conditions necessary for buyer movement from entry to completion are present and correctly calibrated. It is not the same as high conversion rate. A path can have a conversion rate above its category benchmark and still have structural integrity failures — conditions that suppress conversion below what the path's traffic quality and offer quality would otherwise produce. Structural integrity identifies whether the path is performing against its structural potential, not whether it is performing above an external average.

The opposite of structural integrity is structural leakage: the revenue that exits the path not because buyers rejected the offer, but because the architecture of the path failed to sustain buyer movement to the commitment point. Structural leakage is a quantifiable loss that persists regardless of traffic volume, creative quality, or offer adjustment, because its cause is architectural rather than motivational.

The Funnelplugs Standard defines structural integrity across three formal dimensions. Each dimension has a corresponding set of diagnostic criteria and a corresponding set of plug classes — the intervention categories that restore integrity when a dimension is failing. A path that satisfies all three dimensions and holds no active failure classes from the Plug Ontology is classified as structurally sound. A path that fails one or more dimensions has a structural integrity deficit in the specific dimension that is failing — and the classification system identifies both the deficit and the required intervention class with precision.

The three formal integrity dimensions

The Funnel Integrity Standard defines three formal dimensions against which any commercial path can be assessed. These dimensions are not sequential — they operate simultaneously throughout the path. A failure in any dimension at any position in the path is a structural integrity deficit regardless of how well the other dimensions perform at that position.

Trust Integrity. The dimension that governs the presence and calibration of confidence architecture at commitment-proximate positions in the path. Trust Integrity requires that the path provide sufficient proof, legitimacy signals, and risk-reversal architecture to bring buyer confidence above the threshold required for the specific commitment being asked. This threshold is not fixed — it scales with the size and reversibility of the commitment. A path that has excellent trust architecture calibrated to a small commitment is not structurally sound when it asks for a larger commitment without recalibrating. Trust Integrity failure is the most common cause of late-stage abandonment: buyers who exit at the commitment point despite having completed the full preceding path.

Flow Continuity. The dimension that governs the structural capacity of the path to sustain buyer movement from entry to completion without interruption, friction accumulation above threshold, or intent momentum decay. Flow Continuity requires that every stage transition in the path is architecturally complete — that the conditions that end one stage align with the conditions that begin the next, and that the cumulative friction across stages does not exceed the buyer's motivation at the critical conversion positions. Flow Continuity failure manifests as mid-path abandonment that is not concentrated at the decision point but distributed across stage transitions — the signature of a path that loses buyers not through a single critical failure but through progressive attrition.

Recovery Readiness. The dimension that governs the structural presence of re-engagement architecture for buyers who exit the path before converting. Recovery Readiness requires that the path extends the commercial relationship beyond the first session by detecting exit events, classifying abandonment type, and delivering structured re-engagement sequences calibrated to the abandonment context. A path without Recovery Readiness is a one-attempt commercial system — it captures the minority of buyers who convert on the first visit and permanently loses every buyer who does not. Given that research across commercial categories consistently establishes that 60 to 80 percent of eventual buyers do not convert on the first visit, a path without Recovery Readiness is structurally surrendering the majority of its recoverable revenue.

The ten structural failure classes

The Plug Ontology defines ten structural failure classes. Each class names a specific failure condition in the commercial path. Each has a corresponding plug — the intervention class that addresses it structurally. The ten classes are not a spectrum of severity; they are distinct failure types with different causes, different diagnostic signatures, and different corrections.

Trust Infrastructure Failure — The trust-building layer is absent from the path. No architecture exists to build confidence between the entry and the commitment point. Proof Alignment Failure — Proof elements exist but are not positioned where they serve the commitment decision. Proof present at the wrong position does not carry confidence to the threshold where it is needed.

Intent Continuity Failure — The message, context, or audience assumption changes between stages, disrupting the buyer's frame and causing a confidence reset at the transition point. Flow Continuity Failure — The path has architectural gaps or discontinuities that terminate forward movement at stage transitions regardless of buyer motivation level.

Offer-Action Mismatch — The action the path asks the buyer to take is not correctly calibrated to the offer being presented. The commitment architecture does not match the offer architecture. Trust Collapse — Confidence fails at the decision boundary because the confidence architecture was insufficient to carry the buyer across the commitment threshold at that specific position.

Friction Overload — The cumulative friction imposed by the path at a conversion-critical position exceeds the buyer's remaining motivation, terminating movement through resistive force rather than structural interruption. Decision Ambiguity — The path fails to present a clear, singular decision at the conversion moment. Buyers cannot determine what they are committing to, which produces decision paralysis and exit.

Message-Path Misalignment — The message, audience, or intent assumed by the traffic source is not consistent with the message, audience, or intent served by the landing or conversion architecture. Entry intent is not carried through to the commercial path. Recovery Absence — No re-engagement architecture exists. The path is a one-attempt system and makes no structural effort to recover buyers who exit before converting.

The diagnostic methodology

The Funnelplugs Protocol defines the three-stage diagnostic methodology for structural integrity assessment. The Protocol governs the order in which diagnostic work is conducted — not because earlier stages are more important, but because the findings of each stage determine what work in the subsequent stage is necessary and in what sequence.

Stage 1 — Detection. The identification of structural failure signals in path analytics, behavioral data, and architectural audit. Detection work produces a failure signal inventory — the observable evidence that structural leakage is occurring and the positions at which it is concentrated. Detection does not require the application of failure class labels. It produces the raw data against which classification is subsequently conducted.

Stage 2 — Classification. The assignment of detected failure signals to the ten failure classes in the Plug Ontology. Classification work transforms the failure signal inventory into a structured diagnosis — a map of which specific failure classes are active in the path, in what positions, and with what severity. Classification is the step that determines which plug classes are required. Without classification, intervention selection is speculative rather than structural.

Stage 3 — Intervention. The selection, sequencing, and installation of plug classes in the order the classification and prioritization logic requires. Intervention work follows the sequencing principles of the Plug Class Prioritization framework: entry-stage failures before conversion-stage failures, trust before conversion optimization, flow before recovery. The correct intervention sequence is determined by the classification, not by implementation convenience or presumed impact.

The Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine implements a simplified version of the Protocol's Stage 1 and Stage 2 process through a governed 20-question diagnostic. It produces an integrity score, five dimension scores, primary and secondary leak class identifications, and integrity band classification — the starting point for applying the Protocol's Classification and Intervention stages to a specific commercial path.

Self-assessment criteria

The following criteria provide an initial structural assessment of each integrity dimension. They are diagnostic indicators rather than definitive classifications. Positive findings at any criterion indicate that the corresponding failure class may be active in the path and warrant fuller diagnostic work through the Protocol or the Engine.

Trust Integrity assessment: Does late-stage abandonment persist even when price objections appear to be resolved? Do buyers return to commitment-proximate pages multiple times without converting? Is the trust architecture calibrated to the commitment size of the primary offer — not just to the size of a much smaller or much larger prior offer? Are proof elements positioned at the commitment point, or are they concentrated in early-path stages where commitment decisions have not yet been made?

Flow Continuity assessment: Is path abandonment distributed across stage transitions rather than concentrated at a single point? Are there stage transitions where the message, visual system, or assumed audience changes in a way that would be perceptible to the buyer? Does the cumulative friction across the path stay below the motivation level of the marginal buyer at the conversion-critical position — not the buyer's initial motivation level, but the motivation they retain by the time they reach the commitment step? Are there any dead-end states in the path architecture?

Recovery Readiness assessment: Is there an automated email sequence that fires specifically in response to abandonment events — not a general newsletter sequence, but a triggered, classified abandonment response? Is there paid retargeting infrastructure segmented by abandonment position rather than applied uniformly to all site visitors? When a re-engaged buyer responds to a recovery sequence, do they return to the correct position in the commercial path, or to the top of the funnel?
