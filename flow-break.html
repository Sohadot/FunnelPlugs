Central Registry for Funnel Integrity

Flow Break

The condition in which buyer forward momentum is interrupted by a structural discontinuity in the commercial path — not a UX problem, but a failure of the path to maintain continuity between intent and action.

Scope

Defines Flow Break as a classifiable leak condition within Intent Continuity Failure and Flow Continuity Failure in the Plug Ontology. Covers four structural types, the intent-momentum model, and diagnostic methodology.

Structural definition

Flow Break is the condition in which buyer forward momentum is interrupted by a structural discontinuity in the commercial path. The critical precision in this definition is what flow break is not: it is not a page speed problem, not a design aesthetics failure, and not a copywriting weakness. Each of those factors can contribute to drop-off, but they describe surface qualities of individual pages, not the structural relationship between stages.

A flow break is specifically a failure of continuity between a buyer's active intent and the next required action the commercial path demands of them. The buyer arrives at a transition point carrying intent — a motivated disposition toward proceeding. The path presents them with something structurally discontinuous with that intent. The gap between what the buyer's momentum expects and what the path delivers is the break. Momentum cannot sustain itself across a discontinuity; it dissipates at the break point, and with it the probability of conversion.

This distinction matters because the correct intervention for a flow break is structural repair of the discontinuity, not aesthetic improvement of the existing pages. Polishing a broken step does not bridge the gap. The break must be identified, classified, and patched with a structural element — a missing page, a connecting message, a repositioned decision point, or a resolved technical failure — before surface optimization produces meaningful results.

Types of flow break

The Plug Ontology classifies flow breaks into four structural categories, each with a distinct diagnostic signature and a distinct required intervention.

A structural break is a missing page or step in the commercial sequence. The path lacks a necessary transition stage: the buyer is taken from an awareness context directly to a purchase decision without the intervening stages that build orientation and intent. Structural breaks often appear in funnels constructed by backward-mapping from a conversion goal without forward-mapping the buyer's informational and emotional progression. The diagnostic signature is sharp drop-off immediately after a stage transition, where the destination page has a high exit rate despite receiving traffic from an apparently compatible source.

A contextual break is a brand or message mismatch between stages. The buyer arrives at a stage that communicates something inconsistent with what preceded it. The visual identity shifts, the tone changes, the offer framing contradicts the preceding promise, or the assumed knowledge level of the page doesn't match the stage at which the buyer arrived. Contextual breaks are particularly damaging in paid traffic funnels where ad creative and landing page messaging are built by different teams. The diagnostic signature is high traffic from a source with lower-than-expected conversion despite the source being objectively qualified.

A decision break is a premature or delayed ask. The path demands a commitment decision before the buyer has sufficient orientation — the checkout appears before adequate product context — or delays the ask so long that momentum dissipates before it arrives. Decision timing is a structural property of the sequence, not a copy property of individual pages. The diagnostic signature is high engagement on pre-ask content followed by steep drop-off at the ask, or high awareness-stage traffic with low willingness to proceed to the next stage.

A technical break is a functional failure: a broken link, a failed form submission, an unresponsive interactive element, a payment processor error, or a mobile rendering failure that prevents the required action from completing. Technical breaks are the most immediately diagnosable type because they produce near-zero transition rates at specific steps. However, they are also underdiagnosed in funnels without session recording or error monitoring, because standard analytics may show pageviews without revealing that no action was possible.

The intent-momentum model

Understanding why flow breaks cause revenue loss requires understanding the nature of buyer intent. Intent is not a binary state — present or absent. It is a momentum: a directional force that accumulates through positive commercial interactions and dissipates through friction, discontinuity, and elapsed time. Intent has a half-life. The buyer who was ready to proceed five minutes ago may not be ready now. The buyer who was ready on the first page of a sequence may arrive at the fourth page with significantly reduced momentum if the intervening stages failed to sustain it.

Each step in the commercial path has a dual function: it must complete its own informational or persuasive work, and it must capture the buyer's incoming momentum and redirect it toward the next step. A step that completes its own content work but fails to redirect momentum toward the next action is a partial success that still contributes to flow break. The transition between steps — the micro-decision to proceed — is the structural load-bearing point, and it is precisely at transitions that flow breaks occur.

Restoring flow after a break requires two operations, not one. The structural discontinuity must be patched: the missing step added, the contextual mismatch resolved, the decision timing corrected, or the technical failure repaired. But patching the break alone is insufficient if the buyer who reaches that stage arrives with depleted momentum because of earlier friction or elapsed time. A repaired flow break at step four must also account for the momentum state of buyers arriving at step four — which may require upstream adjustments to maintain the intent signal through the earlier stages of the sequence.

Diagnostic signals

Flow break diagnosis requires transition-level analysis rather than aggregate funnel metrics. The primary diagnostic instruments are drop-off rates at specific stage boundaries, page entry-to-next-click rates at each step, session termination position mapping, and traffic source alignment analysis.

Drop-off rates at transitions identify which stage boundaries are losing momentum. A step with a 70% exit rate is not by itself diagnostic — it may be the correct position for buyers who determined the offer wasn't right for them. What matters is whether the exit rate at a specific transition is structurally elevated relative to equivalent stages, and whether it clusters tightly at one transition or distributes across many.

Entry-to-next-click rates measure whether buyers who reach a stage proceed to the next action. A page that is being read but not acted upon is either a decision break (the ask isn't present when momentum peaks) or a contextual break (the content doesn't sustain the intent built upstream).

Session termination positions show where buyers are choosing to stop. If termination clusters at a specific page rather than distributing across the path, a localized structural failure is present at that position. Traffic source misalignment identifies contextual breaks: when a specific traffic source consistently produces lower conversion at a specific stage, the messaging that source carries into the funnel is misaligned with what that stage presents.

Classification position in the ontology

Flow Break is classified within two overlapping failure classes in the Plug Ontology: Intent Continuity Failure and Flow Continuity Failure. Intent Continuity Failure governs the broader class of conditions in which buyer intent is not sustained across the commercial path — of which flow break is the structural, transition-level manifestation. Flow Continuity Failure governs the specific class of conditions in which the path fails to maintain forward movement between stages.

The Flow Continuity Layer of the ontology specifies the structural standards a commercial path must meet to be classified as flow-continuous: no missing transitions, consistent contextual framing across stages, correctly timed decision points, and verified functional integrity at each step. A path that fails any of these standards contains at least one classifiable flow break. The Funnelplugs Standard defines the minimum structural requirements for a commercially viable path, of which flow continuity is a foundational component. Flow Break as a failure condition triggers the corresponding Flow Continuity Plug — the structural intervention that repairs the specific discontinuity type identified through diagnosis.
