Integrity band interpretation

Bands compress multidimensional weakness into prioritized action framing.

Scope

The Integrity Band is the primary output of the Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine — a governed classification that positions a commercial path on a structural completeness spectrum and determines the priority and sequence of intervention. Understanding how to interpret band output correctly is the prerequisite for correct response sequencing. Band misinterpretation produces the most common intervention error: applying optimization to a path that requires structural remediation first.

What the Integrity Band Is

The Integrity Band is the diagnostic classification output of the Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine — a governed range classification that positions a commercial path along a structural completeness spectrum from critically incomplete to structurally sound. It is not a score out of 100. It is not a percentage of some theoretical maximum. It is a structural band: a classification that determines which plug classes are active in the path being diagnosed, how many are active, and which remediation sequence that combination requires.

The distinction between a score and a band is operationally significant. A score implies a continuous improvement metric — a path scoring 72 is better than one scoring 68, and the goal is to increase the number. A band implies a structural classification — a path in the Compromised Band has a specific set of active failure classes that require a specific intervention sequence. Moving from Compromised to Marginal is not about incrementally increasing a number. It is about identifying which specific plug classes are absent and installing them, at which point the path structurally reclassifies.

The Integrity Band output is deterministic. The engine evaluates the presence or absence of structural elements corresponding to each of the 10 plug classes in the Plug Ontology. The combination of absent plug classes maps directly to a band classification. There is no subjective scoring — the band is a function of what structural elements the diagnostic finds present or absent in the path.

Band classification is the prerequisite for all subsequent intervention decisions. The band determines not just what is wrong with the path, but what category of response is appropriate. Proceeding to optimization without running a diagnostic — without knowing the band classification — is the structural equivalent of administering a treatment without a diagnosis.

The Four Band Classifications

The Integrity Band system classifies paths into four structural categories. Each band has a defined set of diagnostic criteria, a characterization of the path's structural state, and a prescribed response priority.

Critical Band is the classification for paths in which multiple fundamental plug classes are absent. The path is structurally unsound: its stage sequence has significant gaps, its alignment mechanisms are absent or severely compromised, and its decision architecture is insufficient for the commitment levels being requested. Revenue destruction is systemic rather than localized — buyers are being lost at multiple structural failure points throughout the path, not at isolated weak spots. A Critical Band path cannot be meaningfully improved by optimization. Every optimization dollar deployed into a Critical Band path is consumed by structural leakage before it produces results. The prescribed response is structural rebuild: addressing the missing plug classes before any optimization activity is initiated.

Compromised Band is the classification for paths in which two to three plug classes are absent. The path has significant structural gaps, but it is not comprehensively broken. Some stages are structurally sound; others have identifiable plug class deficiencies that are producing predictable, patterned revenue loss. Compromised Band paths have a recognizable leak pattern: the same structural failure points are producing consistent buyer loss in a repeating sequence. Because the failure is patterned rather than systemic, it is diagnosable and addressable through a specific plug installation sequence. The prescribed response is plug installation before optimization — identifying the two to three absent plug classes, installing them in the correct sequence, and confirming structural reclassification before applying optimization resources.

Marginal Band is the classification for paths in which one to two plug classes are absent. The path is functionally operational — buyers can move through it and a baseline conversion rate exists — but it has specific structural weaknesses that are producing revenue loss above what optimization can address. Marginal Band paths often present as optimization-resistant: campaigns, copy tests, and offer adjustments produce limited uplift because there is a structural deficiency that is limiting conversion at a level below what the optimization is targeting. The prescribed response is targeted plug installation alongside selective optimization — the absent plug classes are installed, while optimization is applied to the stages that are already structurally sound.

Sound Band is the classification for paths in which no active plug classes are identified — all required structural elements are present, correctly sequenced, and sufficiently complete for the path to meet the structural completeness threshold defined in the Funnel Integrity Standard. A Sound Band path has no structural leaks that require plug installation. Buyers who exit are doing so for reasons that optimization can address: offer appeal, price sensitivity, competitive alternatives, or stage-level performance issues. The prescribed response is optimization without structural intervention — the path is structurally ready for performance improvement work.

What Band Determines

The Integrity Band does not merely describe the state of the path. It determines the entire response architecture: what category of intervention is appropriate, in what sequence it should be applied, and at what point optimization becomes a legitimate investment rather than a structural waste.

Critical Band determines that structural rebuild precedes everything. No optimization. No campaign scaling. No offer testing. The path is structurally unsound, and additional traffic or conversion optimization applied to a structurally unsound path produces no durable results. The intervention sequence begins with identifying which plug classes are absent, prioritizing them by impact on buyer progression, and building them in the correct order. Optimization is deferred until the path reclassifies out of Critical.

Compromised Band determines that a plug installation sequence precedes optimization. The two to three absent plug classes are identified, a sequence for their installation is established based on their position in the commercial path, and they are installed before optimization resources are deployed. Optimization work runs after structural reclassification to Marginal or Sound — not in parallel with it.

Marginal Band determines that targeted plug installation runs alongside selective optimization. Because the path is functionally operational with only one to two structural deficiencies, optimization is not purely wasted — stages that are structurally sound can benefit from performance improvement while the absent plug classes are being addressed. The constraint is that optimization should be applied selectively to structurally sound stages, not to the stages adjacent to or containing the identified structural deficiencies.

Sound Band determines that optimization is the appropriate and exclusive intervention. No plug installation is required. Structural completeness has been confirmed. The path's performance ceiling is determined by optimization variables — copy, offer, targeting, pricing, creative — rather than by structural gaps. All resources can be directed at performance improvement without structural concerns limiting the return on that investment.

Why Band Classification Matters

The practical importance of band classification is its prevention of the most expensive error in commercial path management: applying optimization resources to a structurally broken path.

When a Critical Band path receives optimization investment — paid traffic scaling, creative testing, offer refinement, email sequence optimization — that investment does not compound. The structural leaks consume the incremental buyers that optimization delivers. The conversion rate does not improve sustainably because buyers are leaving the path at structural gaps that optimization cannot address. The investment is wasted not because the optimization was poor, but because it was applied to the wrong problem category.

Band classification prevents this error by making the structural state of the path explicit before any investment decision is made. A team that knows their path is in the Critical Band will not scale paid traffic. A team that knows their path is in the Sound Band will not commission a structural audit before running their next campaign. The band aligns the intervention category with the path's actual structural state.

The value of band classification is proportional to the investment being directed at the path. For high-traffic, high-investment commercial paths, the cost of misclassification — of treating a Critical Band path as optimization-ready — can be substantial. The band is not administrative overhead. It is the mechanism by which investment decisions are correctly categorized and resources are directed to their highest-value use.

Using the Engine

The Integrity Band is produced by the Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine — the diagnostic tool that evaluates commercial path structural completeness against the 10 plug classes in the Plug Ontology. The engine's output includes the band classification, the specific plug classes identified as absent or compromised, and the prescribed intervention sequence for the path's current band.

The diagnostic protocol governing how the engine is applied — the sequence in which path elements are evaluated, the criteria for each plug class assessment, and the rules for band assignment — is defined in the Protocol. The structural standards against which each plug class is evaluated are defined in the Plug Ontology. Running the engine correctly requires understanding both the diagnostic protocol and the ontology — the protocol defines how to evaluate, and the ontology defines what to evaluate against.
