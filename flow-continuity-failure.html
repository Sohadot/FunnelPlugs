Plug Ontology v1.0 — Class 04

Flow Continuity Failure

Movement from intent to completion is available in the path but not structurally supported. The path contains breaks, competing exits, and momentum gaps that allow forward progress to terminate before the conversion event.

Class overview

Flow Continuity Failure is the structural condition in which a commercial path contains interruptions, competing navigation options, or momentum breaks that allow buyers to exit before completing the conversion sequence. The path is technically complete but structurally unable to hold buyers in forward motion.

Formal definition

The commercial path contains structural interruptions, competing navigation options, or momentum breaks that allow the buyer's forward progress to stall or terminate before reaching the conversion event. The path does not hold the buyer in motion between stages. Movement from intent to completion is available but not structurally supported.

Structural meaning

Flow Continuity Failure is structural because it persists even when the offer is sound, the trust architecture is adequate, and the decision is clear. The buyer is willing to proceed. The path is not designed to keep them proceeding. Competing navigation options within checkout sequences, unguided stage transitions, and multi-step flows without progress support are structural gaps that produce exits at commercially preventable moments. The failure is in the path architecture, not in buyer intent.

Where it appears

Throughout multi-step paths: checkout sequences, application flows, onboarding journeys, and lead nurture paths. Particularly present at stage transitions where the buyer must initiate the next step without structural guidance, or where external navigation options compete with the intended forward direction. Also present in paths where the number of required steps is not communicated, leaving buyers uncertain whether the end is near or far.

What it damages

Path completion rate, step-to-step conversion, and the compounding investment made in bringing the buyer to each stage. Flow Continuity Failure wastes all upstream acquisition and engagement investment by allowing exits at structurally preventable moments. The impact is proportionate to the position in the path: exits earlier in multi-step flows waste more upstream cost than exits near completion.

Typical symptoms

High drop-off rates at specific, identifiable steps with no corresponding technical failure. Buyers reaching the middle of a multi-step flow without completing it. Checkout abandonment concentrated at one or two consistent internal steps. Exit to homepage or site navigation from within an active conversion sequence. Form abandonment on non-required fields or at stage boundaries. Significant variance in completion rate between sessions where the buyer is interrupted versus uninterrupted sessions.

Diagnostic logic

Map every stage transition in multi-step conversion sequences. At each transition, identify the structural mechanisms that guide buyers to the next step versus the exit opportunities available. Quantify the proportion of buyers who reach each step without completing it. Identify whether exit patterns cluster at specific transitions, suggesting structural gap points, versus distributing evenly, suggesting a different failure class. Competing navigation access within conversion sequences is a structural signal for this class.

Required plug logic

Stage-by-stage flow audit mapping every structural exit opportunity within conversion sequences. Removal or suppression of competing navigation options inside conversion-critical paths. Progress indicators where multi-step completion is required, setting buyer expectations about remaining effort. Structural micro-commitment design to maintain forward momentum through stage transitions. The intervention addresses the architecture of forward motion, not the content of any single step.

Intervention class: Flow Continuity Layer

Relationship to the Funnel Integrity Standard

Flow Continuity is the Funnel Integrity Standard dimension that Flow Continuity Failure directly tests against. The standard requires that the path not break, stall, or create avoidable interruption between intent and completion. When the structural design of the path creates the conditions for avoidable interruption, this dimension is violated.

Primary standard dimension: Flow Continuity

Relationship to the FunnelPlugs Protocol

The FunnelPlugs Protocol identifies Flow Continuity Failure through two stages. Detection maps path zones and identifies step-level drop-off patterns. Classification assigns the failure to this class based on the location, clustering, and architectural characteristics of the exit points identified.

Protocol stage: Detection and Classification

Relationship to the Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine

Flow Continuity Failure maps to the Flow Break leak class in the Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine's diagnostic taxonomy. Declared step-drop signals, navigation exit patterns, multi-step abandonment data, and sequential incompletion rates route diagnostic output toward this class.

Relationship to the Plug Registry

The Plug Registry's Flow Continuity Layer is the corresponding intervention category. Registry entries in this category define the structural mechanisms for maintaining forward momentum: path simplification, navigation containment, progress communication, and stage-transition design.

Representative diagnostic scenario

Representative diagnostic scenario — not client data.

An ecommerce path uses a four-step checkout sequence: cart review, shipping details, payment details, order confirmation. The cart review step and the shipping details step are contained within the checkout flow with minimal navigation access. The payment details step, however, is rendered in a page environment that retains the full site header and footer navigation. At this step, buyers have access to the full site menu, promotional links, and a site-wide search bar. A measurable proportion of buyers who reach the payment step navigate away using these elements rather than completing the transaction. They do not return. The structural failure is not in the payment form or the offer — both are technically sound. It is in the navigation architecture of the payment step, which provides structural exit opportunities at the highest-commitment moment in the path.

Claim boundary

This class identifies a structural failure condition. It does not estimate revenue recovery, predict conversion lift, or guarantee performance improvement. Commercial impact is described qualitatively. Representative scenarios are illustrative and are not drawn from client engagements. No claim on this page constitutes a performance promise.
