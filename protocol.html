Identification and Intervention Protocol

A governed three-stage methodology for diagnosing where commercial flow breaks, classifying the underlying failure mode against the Plug Ontology, and assigning the structurally correct intervention layer.

Protocol role

The Protocol is the methodological engine of the Funnelplugs system. It converts structural doctrine into a repeatable diagnostic process: a sequence of governed steps that takes a practitioner from an observation of commercial underperformance to a precise, classified failure assignment and an intervention logic grounded in structural reasoning rather than intuition. The Protocol's Classification stage operates exclusively against the Plug Ontology—each detected failure is assigned to one of ten structural failure classes before any intervention is designed. The Standard against which the path is measured is the Funnel Integrity Standard, which defines the three integrity dimensions a sound commercial path must satisfy.

Why structure determines diagnosis

The dominant failure mode in commercial path diagnosis is not inaccuracy. It is imprecision. Teams identify that conversion is low, that drop-off is occurring, that buyers are not completing. The observation is correct. The response is structural guesswork: a new headline, a revised call to action, a different image, a variation of the offer. The metric shifts slightly within the noise band. The underlying condition persists. Six weeks later, a different iteration of the same intervention is tested.

This cycle exists because diagnosis is treating symptoms as if they were the disease. Symptoms are observable. The structural condition producing them is not directly visible — it requires a governed framework to surface. A symptom tells you that something is wrong. A structural classification tells you what is wrong, where it is, and what category of intervention addresses it. Without the latter, every response is a guess dressed as a decision.

The FunnelPlugs Protocol is designed to break this cycle. Its foundational premise is that commercial paths fail for structural reasons — not random ones — and that structural failure is classifiable with sufficient precision to direct intervention before testing begins. The Protocol does not eliminate iteration, but it eliminates uninformed iteration. When a failure is classified as Trust Infrastructure Failure at the commitment stage, the intervention set narrows to legitimacy layer construction at that specific stage. Testing within that set is rational. Testing outside it is cost without purpose.

Structure matters for diagnosis because commercial paths are systems, and systems fail in systematic ways. The same structural conditions that produce checkout abandonment in a B2C e-commerce path produce the same signal pattern in a B2B lead capture path, because both paths are governed by the same underlying integrity requirements: trust must be established before commitment is asked for, flow must be maintained between stages, and recovery mechanisms must exist for exits the active path cannot prevent. Structural diagnosis finds the common condition beneath the surface variation.

Stage 01 — Detection

Mapping the commercial path against the integrity standard

Detection is the systematic process of mapping an active commercial path against the Funnel Integrity Standard to identify zones where required structural conditions are absent, degraded, or misaligned. It is not a conversion rate audit. Detection does not begin with performance metrics and work backward. It begins with the path architecture and works forward through each stage, asking a precise question at each point: does this stage satisfy the integrity conditions the Standard requires?

The three integrity dimensions — Trust Integrity, Flow Continuity, and Recovery Readiness — serve as the diagnostic lens for each stage of the path. Detection is complete when every stage of the path has been assessed against all three dimensions, and every zone of structural absence or failure has been located and documented.

  • What Detection looks at

    Detection maps the entire commercial path — from the first point of buyer contact through to the post-conversion layer — as a stage-by-stage structural inventory. Each stage is assessed against three sets of integrity questions corresponding to the Standard's dimensions.

    For Trust Integrity: Does this stage provide the signals, proof layers, and legitimacy markers appropriate to the level of commitment being asked? Where the buyer is asked to submit payment credentials, sign up with a work email, or take a high-commitment action, does the path present adequate trust infrastructure immediately preceding and adjacent to that action — or does it proceed directly to the ask without establishing the preconditions for trust?

    For Flow Continuity: Does this stage maintain forward momentum without structural interruption? Are competing navigation options present within conversion-critical sequences that allow buyers to exit the intended path direction? Does the transition from this stage to the next carry the buyer's intent and context forward coherently, or does it reset to a generic brand framing that loses the intent signal established earlier?

    For Recovery Readiness: When a buyer exits this stage without converting, does the system have a governed response? Is that response calibrated to the specific stage of exit, or is it a generic re-engagement sequence that re-enters the path at the beginning rather than at the abandonment point?

  • Detection signals and indicators

    Detection uses two categories of signal: structural signals and behavioral signals. Both are necessary. Neither alone is sufficient.

    Structural signals are observable directly from the path architecture, without reference to performance data. Examples: a checkout page with no visible security indicators or third-party validation — a structural Trust Integrity gap. A multi-step form flow that provides no progress indicator and no suppression of global navigation — a structural Flow Continuity gap. A cart abandonment event with no recovery sequence triggered within 24 hours — a structural Recovery Readiness gap. These conditions are identifiable through path audit alone. They represent structural failure regardless of what the conversion rate happens to be at that moment.

    Behavioral signals are derived from measurement — stage-level drop-off data, session analysis, heatmaps, form abandonment rates, exit page distributions. They serve as locators: they tell you where structural examination should focus. High exit rates at a specific checkout step direct Detection to examine that step against the integrity dimensions. Extended session time on a pricing page without action directs Detection to examine Decision Clarity conditions at that surface. Behavioral signals do not replace structural audit; they prioritize it.

    A Detection finding is not a conversion rate. It is a structural statement: at stage X, the Trust Integrity dimension is not satisfied because legitimacy signals are absent from the commitment surface. That statement is the input to Classification.

  • What Detection looks like in practice

    A practitioner running Detection on a lead generation funnel works through the path in sequence. At the entry stage — the landing page receiving paid traffic — they assess whether message continuity exists between the traffic source and the page: does the landing surface speak to the specific intent signal declared by the search query or ad creative that drove the visit, or does it present a generic brand position? If the latter, Detection records an intent alignment gap at the entry stage.

    At the form submission stage — the point at which the visitor is asked to provide name, email, and company — Detection assesses trust infrastructure: are there social proof signals, security indicators, or third-party validation markers present adjacent to the form? If the form is presented without any trust signals, Detection records a trust infrastructure gap at the lead capture stage.

    After the form submission event, Detection examines the recovery layer: is there a sequence that re-engages visitors who loaded the form page but did not submit? Is there a sequence for visitors who began filling the form but abandoned it? If neither exists, Detection records a recovery absence across the lead capture path.

    Three findings. Three structural locations. No performance metric required to identify any of them — though behavioral data from the Revenue Leak Engine can prioritize which finding is likely the most consequential given the path's current conversion pattern.

Stage 02 — Classification

Assigning failure to governed ontology classes before intervention begins

Classification is the stage that separates structural diagnosis from informed guesswork. It takes each Detection finding — a located structural gap at a specific path stage against a specific integrity dimension — and assigns it to a governed class in the Plug Ontology. The Ontology defines ten structural failure classes, each with a precise definition, a characteristic symptom pattern, and a required intervention logic. Classification is the process of matching findings to classes with sufficient precision to direct intervention.

The importance of Classification before Intervention cannot be overstated. The most common failure in funnel remediation is applying the right intervention to the wrong failure class. Trust Infrastructure Failure and Proof Alignment Failure both manifest as hesitation at conversion stages. Both appear to involve trust. They require different responses. Trust Infrastructure Failure requires building the legitimacy layer — installing signals of security, authority, and third-party validation. Proof Alignment Failure requires repositioning existing proof assets to address the specific hesitation active at the stage where they appear. Treating Proof Alignment Failure with more trust signals is a Category Error: the signals exist and are not the structural problem. Classification prevents this error by requiring precise class assignment before any intervention is designed.

  • How to map symptoms to ontology classes

    Classification uses three pieces of information from Detection: the stage at which failure was located, the integrity dimension that is not being satisfied, and the specific symptom pattern observed. Together these three inputs narrow the possible class assignments and distinguish between classes that share surface similarity.

    Stage location is the first filter. Recovery Absence Failure is a post-path condition — it manifests after buyers exit without converting. If a Detection finding is located within the active conversion path rather than in the post-path layer, Recovery Absence Failure is not the correct class regardless of how the symptoms appear. Similarly, Commitment Boundary Failure is localized to the final conversion action — the moment of execution. If the failure is occurring earlier in the path during offer evaluation, the more likely class is Decision Clarity Failure. Stage narrows the candidate set.

    Integrity dimension is the second filter. A finding against the Trust Integrity dimension routes toward Trust Infrastructure Failure, Decision Clarity Failure, Commitment Boundary Failure, or Proof Alignment Failure. A finding against the Flow Continuity dimension routes toward Intent Continuity Failure, Flow Continuity Failure, Offer-Action Mismatch, or Measurement Blindness Failure. A finding against Recovery Readiness routes directly to Recovery Absence Failure. Dimension narrows further.

    Symptom pattern makes the final determination within the remaining candidate classes. High final-step abandonment with strong prior engagement, combined with post-conversion reversal rates, indicates Commitment Boundary Failure rather than Decision Clarity Failure — the buyer understood the offer, reached the execution moment, and reversed. Extended evaluation time on a pricing page followed by exit without action, combined with support queries asking for clarification on offer terms, indicates Decision Clarity Failure — the buyer could not evaluate the offer with sufficient confidence. Symptom pattern is the diagnostic that resolves class ambiguity.

  • Why classification before intervention is structurally mandatory

    Every structural intervention has a cost: design time, implementation time, copy time, technical deployment, and the opportunity cost of every other intervention not being pursued. An intervention applied to the wrong failure class does not merely fail to solve the structural condition — it consumes the resources that would have addressed it correctly, delays the actual fix, and produces a data record that appears to say "this intervention did not work," when the accurate reading is "this intervention was applied to the wrong failure class."

    Classification is not a bureaucratic requirement. It is the structural precondition for efficient intervention. A classified failure has an address — Trust Infrastructure Failure at the checkout stage — and that address connects directly to a defined intervention logic: the trust infrastructure plug class. The practitioner does not need to generate hypotheses about what might work. The Ontology provides the governed intervention logic for each class. Classification converts an open-ended remediation problem into a constrained, directed intervention task.

    The ten classes in the Plug Ontology are designed to cover the full range of structural failure conditions that produce revenue leakage in commercial paths. If a Detection finding cannot be assigned to one of the ten classes, the finding is likely a symptom description rather than a structural diagnosis. The correct response is to refine the Detection finding — to locate it more precisely against the integrity dimension and the stage — until the class assignment becomes possible.

Stage 03 — Intervention

Selecting the correct plug class and distinguishing structural response from cosmetic response

Intervention is the design and deployment of the structural correction required by the classified failure. It is not the execution of tactics. It is the installation of a plug — the class of structural mechanism required to address the failure mode that Classification has identified. The Plug Registry organizes governed intervention categories by failure class, providing the practitioner with the defined structural response for each classified condition.

The governing principle of the Intervention stage is structural correction at the failure point. Intervention does not redesign the entire path because one stage has failed. It addresses the specific structural gap identified by Detection and classified by Classification, at the specific stage where it exists, with the intervention logic the Ontology prescribes for that class.

  • Plug class selection logic

    Each failure class in the Ontology maps to a defined plug class — the category of structural mechanism that addresses that failure mode. The selection is not a creative exercise. It is a governed lookup: classified failure class determines plug class. Decision Clarity Failure routes to the Decision Guidance Layer plug class. Flow Continuity Failure routes to the Flow Continuity Layer plug class. Recovery Absence Failure routes to the Abandonment Recovery Logic plug class.

    Plug class selection determines the type of structural work required, not the specific implementation. Trust Infrastructure plug class work requires building legitimacy layer elements — credential display, security signals, third-party validation markers — at the specific commitment stage where the failure was located. The exact implementation varies by path type, audience, and offer structure. The plug class governs the structural function the implementation must serve. The practitioner governs the implementation that serves it.

    Where multiple failure classes have been identified across different path stages, the Intervention stage must determine sequencing. The Protocol's sequencing logic prioritizes failure classes in this order: Measurement Blindness Failure is addressed first, because active measurement is required to verify that any subsequent intervention has been effective. System Ownership Failure is addressed in parallel, because governance gaps will cause every other intervention to degrade over time. Failure classes at later stages in the commercial path are generally addressed before failure classes at earlier stages, because a working path through the first four stages is wasted investment if the final stage cannot close.

  • What makes an intervention structural versus cosmetic

    A structural intervention changes the architecture of the commercial path at the failure point. A cosmetic intervention changes the surface presentation without addressing the structural condition beneath it.

    The distinction is determined by whether the intervention addresses the classified failure class or the observed symptom. Adding more testimonials to a page in response to low checkout conversion is cosmetic if the failure class is Decision Clarity Failure — the buyer cannot evaluate the offer, and additional social proof does not resolve that condition. Adding more testimonials to a page in response to the same conversion rate is structural if the failure class is Trust Infrastructure Failure and the legitimacy layer at that stage is genuinely absent — because the intervention then addresses the classified condition directly.

    The same intervention — adding testimonials — can be structural or cosmetic depending entirely on whether the failure class was determined before the intervention was selected. This is why Classification is the defining stage of the Protocol. The quality of the intervention is a function of the quality of the classification. An incorrectly classified failure will produce an intervention that is cosmetic by definition, regardless of the care with which it is executed.

    Structural interventions share a common characteristic: their mechanism of action maps directly to the failure mechanism they are correcting. A recovery sequence that re-enters the path at the exact abandonment stage addresses Recovery Absence Failure structurally, because the intervention provides the mechanism — governed re-engagement at the correct stage — that the failure mode is defined by the absence of. A generic re-engagement email that sends buyers to the homepage is cosmetic: it responds to the abandonment event without addressing the structural gap the abandonment revealed.

The Protocol's relationship to the Standard and the Ontology

The Protocol, the Funnel Integrity Standard, and the Plug Ontology are three interlocking components of a single diagnostic system. Each performs a function the others cannot perform without it.

The Standard defines the positive specification: what a structurally sound commercial path looks like when it is working correctly. Its three integrity dimensions — Trust Integrity, Flow Continuity, Recovery Readiness — describe the conditions that must be sustained at each stage of the path. The Standard is the reference state. It describes the target.

The Ontology defines the negative specification: the ten structural failure classes that correspond to the absence of each integrity condition. Where the Standard asks "is trust integrity satisfied here?" the Ontology provides the named structural conditions for what it means when it is not: Trust Infrastructure Failure, Decision Clarity Failure, Commitment Boundary Failure, Proof Alignment Failure. The Ontology converts the Standard's dimensions into a classification vocabulary. It is the vocabulary of structural failure.

The Protocol is the process that uses both. Detection uses the Standard's integrity dimensions as the lens for path examination. Classification uses the Ontology's failure classes as the assignment vocabulary for what Detection finds. Intervention uses the plug class logic the Ontology prescribes for each failure class as the direction for structural response. Without the Standard, Detection has no reference state to measure against. Without the Ontology, Classification has no governed vocabulary to assign to findings. Without the Protocol, the Standard and the Ontology are reference documents without an operational process connecting them to live diagnostic work.

The Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine operationalizes this three-component system for practitioners who need diagnostic output without conducting the full manual Protocol. The Engine maps declared funnel conditions against the Ontology through a structured question set, produces a primary failure class assignment, and generates the intervention priority logic the Protocol would produce through manual Classification. The Engine does not replace the Protocol — it applies the Protocol's logic to a declared set of conditions in an automated diagnostic sequence.

What the Protocol is not

The Protocol is frequently positioned alongside methodologies it does not resemble in structure or purpose. Clarifying these distinctions is part of its definition.

The Protocol is not A/B testing. A/B testing is a statistical tool for determining which of two surface variations performs better under a defined measurement condition. It answers the question: "Which version converts more?" The Protocol answers a different question: "What structural condition is preventing this path from performing at its potential?" A/B testing operates within a surface layer. The Protocol operates at the structural layer beneath it. A/B testing is appropriate after Classification — once the failure class is determined and the intervention logic is defined, testing within the intervention class may be rational. A/B testing before Classification is testing without a structural theory: running variations without a governed basis for predicting what will work and why.

The Protocol is not a heuristic checklist. Heuristic checklists — "best practices" for landing pages, checkout flows, email sequences — are collections of observations that correlated with better performance across various path types and historical contexts. They have no structural logic that specifies when they apply, at which stage, under which failure conditions, or in what order. The Protocol's findings are grounded in the Standard's integrity dimensions and assigned to Ontology classes with defined intervention logic. Heuristics suggest what to try. The Protocol specifies what to address and why.

The Protocol is not conversion rate optimization in the conventional sense. CRO, as typically practiced, begins with a performance gap and generates hypotheses to close it. The hypothesis-generation process is often intuitive, experience-based, and disconnected from a structural theory of why the gap exists. The Protocol begins with the structural architecture of the path and generates findings from a governed examination of that architecture against formal integrity conditions. CRO asks: "What might improve performance?" The Protocol asks: "What structural condition is producing underperformance, and what class of intervention addresses that condition?"

The Protocol is not guesswork with process. A three-step process that produces intuitive answers at each step is still guesswork — it is guesswork with ritual. The Protocol's value is not its sequence. Its value is that each stage is governed: Detection is governed by the Standard's integrity dimensions, Classification is governed by the Ontology's failure classes, and Intervention is governed by the plug class logic the Ontology prescribes. Remove the governing documents and the Protocol becomes a structured label for informed intuition. With them, it is a diagnostic methodology with defensible outputs.

The Protocol exists because structural commercial failure is systematic, and systematic failure requires a systematic diagnostic methodology to address it with precision. Not optimization. Not iteration. Diagnosis first — then the correct intervention.
