Diagnostic signal layers

Behavioral signals are governed by interpretation architecture — not read by instinct. The layer system is what separates diagnosis from guesswork.

Scope

This concept defines the structured collection and interpretation architecture that maps behavioral data to structural failure class identification. Diagnostic signal layers support the Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine and are prerequisite to any governed classification under the Funnelplugs Protocol.

What diagnostic signal layers are

Diagnostic signal layers are the structured collection and interpretation architecture that maps behavioral data to structural failure class identification within a commercial path. This is a critical distinction: signal layers are not analytics reports, traffic dashboards, or conversion summaries. They are the governed interpretation system — the methodology — that translates raw behavioral signals into structural diagnoses.

A funnel generates continuous behavioral output: time-on-page, scroll depth, form abandonment, click sequences, return visits, exit timing. Without an interpretation architecture, this data is observational noise. With a governed layer system, each data point is classified by its diagnostic significance — which structural absence it evidences, which failure class it indicates, and which intervention it warrants.

The layer system operates at three levels of signal resolution: entry-level signals (what arrives and how), flow-level signals (how movement progresses or stalls), and exit-level signals (when and where departure occurs). Each level maps to a distinct range of structural failure classes within the Plug Ontology. A diagnostic without all three layers is an incomplete diagnostic — capable of identifying some failure classes but structurally blind to others.

The governed nature of the system ensures that signal interpretation is auditable and repeatable. Two analysts applying the same layer methodology to the same behavioral dataset will reach the same structural classification. This repeatability is what separates a diagnostic system from a subjective opinion about why a funnel is underperforming.

The signal-to-class mapping logic

The diagnostic value of behavioral signals depends entirely on the mapping logic that connects signal patterns to structural failure classes. Raw signals do not self-classify — the architecture supplies the classification rules. The governing principle is this: structural absences produce distinctive behavioral fingerprints, and identifying the correct failure class requires knowing which fingerprint belongs to which class.

Trust Collapse produces a specific fingerprint: engagement is present, but offer acceptance is absent. Visitors read, scroll, and dwell — but conversion at the point of offer is suppressed. The behavioral signal is not low traffic and not high bounce. It is active engagement that terminates at the trust threshold. No amount of traffic optimization resolves this. The correct diagnosis requires recognizing the fingerprint as Trust Collapse, not as a traffic quality problem.

Friction Overload produces a different fingerprint: mid-funnel dropout concentrated at form or checkout steps, with dwell time at those steps significantly above the baseline for other path positions. The signal is not early abandonment — visitors have already moved through entry stages. The dropout is structural, located at the friction point, and repeatable across sessions.

Decision Ambiguity produces extended dwell time with no forward progression. Visitors are present but paralyzed — the structural absence is clarity and decision architecture, not trust or friction. Recovery Absence produces clean post-abandonment exits with no re-entry signal. The behavioral fingerprint is permanent loss — not delayed conversion. Each class has a recognizable signal signature, and the mapping logic governs the correspondence between signature and class identification.

Signal layer architecture

The three-layer architecture organizes diagnostic signals by their position in the commercial path and their corresponding failure class relevance.

Layer 1 — Entry signals govern the diagnostic assessment of what enters the path and whether it is structurally qualified to convert. Entry signals include traffic source classification, source-to-path alignment indicators, intent match scores, and audience temperature indicators. Misalignment at entry produces behavioral patterns that can be mistaken for mid-funnel failure — but the structural cause is upstream. Entry signal analysis isolates Message-Path Misalignment and Audience Qualification Failure before those conditions contaminate flow-level interpretation. Ontology classes most strongly indicated by entry signal analysis include Message-Path Misalignment and Trust Collapse at the offer threshold.

Layer 2 — Flow signals govern the diagnostic assessment of how movement progresses or stalls within the path. Flow signals include step-level dropout rates, time-pattern analysis at each step, hesitation indicators (scroll reversals, repeated element engagement, form interaction without submission), and progressive engagement depth. Flow signal analysis identifies where structural failure is occurring — not just that conversion did not happen, but at which step, with what behavioral signature, and with what class-level correspondence. Ontology classes most strongly indicated by flow signal analysis include Friction Overload, Decision Ambiguity, and Flow Break.

Layer 3 — Exit signals govern the diagnostic assessment of departure — when it occurs, from which position, and whether re-entry follows. Exit signals include abandonment timing relative to path position, final page position before exit, exit page behavioral patterns, and re-entry rate and timing. Exit signals are particularly diagnostic for Recovery Absence: when exits produce no re-entry signal and no recovery path engagement, the structural absence is the recovery layer itself. Exit signals also contextualize flow-level dropout — distinguishing temporary exits from terminal abandonment. The dimensional weakness mapping process draws heavily on all three signal layers to construct the cross-dimensional diagnostic picture.

What measurement blindness looks like

Measurement Blindness is the failure class that occurs when diagnostic signal layers are absent, incomplete, or operating only at session resolution. It is classified as a failure class within the Plug Ontology because its structural consequence is identical to other failure classes: revenue loss with no corrective path.

The indicators of Measurement Blindness are specific: session-level analytics without step-level breakdown, no behavioral segmentation by source or intent, inability to isolate dropout location within the path, and no behavioral fingerprint data that would enable class identification. The commercial path produces revenue loss, but the diagnostic surface does not exist. Every intervention becomes a guess — the team cannot know whether the problem is Trust Collapse or Friction Overload or Decision Ambiguity because no layer architecture has translated behavior into signal, and no signal has been mapped to class.

Measurement Blindness compounds over time. Without signal layers in place, each optimization cycle is executed without structural validation. Changes are made, results are observed at session level, and no causal relationship between structural intervention and behavioral outcome can be confirmed. The path remains structurally incomplete while consuming optimization resources. Installing diagnostic signal layers is itself a structural intervention — the prerequisite for all other diagnoses under the Protocol.

Classification and system position

Diagnostic signal layers occupy a foundational position within the Funnelplugs system. They are the data-collection and interpretation prerequisite for every downstream classification, mapping, and intervention decision. Without governed signal layers, the Plug Ontology cannot be applied — structural failure classes cannot be identified from behavioral noise alone.

The Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine accepts signal inputs and applies the mapping logic to surface structural failure class candidates. The Protocol governs how signal evidence is weighted in classification decisions. The dimensional weakness mapping process uses all three signal layers to construct the cross-dimensional structural picture that enables multi-class diagnosis. In every case, the signal layer architecture is the input stage — the governed translation of behavior into diagnostic language.

For commercial paths currently operating without structured signal layers, the first-priority intervention is not offer optimization or copy revision — it is the installation of a diagnostic signal architecture capable of producing class-level evidence. Revenue recovery cannot be governed without evidence. Evidence cannot be produced without a signal layer system.
