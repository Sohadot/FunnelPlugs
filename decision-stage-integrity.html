Central Registry for Funnel Integrity

Decision-stage integrity

Structural completeness at every commitment-required position — not persuasion, but the architecture that makes a decision possible.

DIMENSION Trust Integrity + Flow Continuity PROTOCOL STAGE Classification PLUG CLASS Decision Guidance Layer

Scope

Decision Stage Integrity defines the structural conditions a commercial path must maintain at every position where a commitment or meaningful choice is required. It connects Decision Ambiguity — the failure class — to the governed structural requirements that prevent and correct it.

What Decision Stage Integrity Is

Decision Stage Integrity is the structural condition in which the commercial path maintains coherence, clarity, and trust-sufficient architecture at every decision-critical position. A decision-critical position is any point in the path where the buyer is asked to commit — to a purchase, to a subscription, to providing personal information, to advancing to a higher-commitment stage. These positions are structurally distinct from informational or engagement positions: they require not just that the buyer understand the offer, but that the buyer be able to form and execute a commitment decision.

Decision Stage Integrity is not about persuasion. It does not address the strength of the argument for the offer, the appeal of the creative, or the credibility of the testimonials. It addresses whether the structural conditions for a decision are present. A path can be persuasive while lacking integrity — the buyer is attracted to the offer but cannot complete the commitment because the path does not provide the structural scaffolding required to execute one. Conversely, a path with strong decision stage integrity does not require exceptional persuasion to convert — the structural completeness of the decision moment reduces friction sufficiently that buyers who want the offer can act on that want.

This distinction is practically significant because it determines what must be fixed first. Persuasive interventions applied to a decision stage that lacks structural integrity are misallocated. The path must first achieve structural completeness at each commitment-required position before persuasive optimization can function as intended. Decision Stage Integrity is the precondition, not the outcome, of conversion optimization.

What a Decision Stage Requires

Five structural elements are required at every decision stage for that stage to achieve integrity. The absence of any single element constitutes a structural failure — not an optimization gap, but a completeness gap that the path must address before further work proceeds.

A clear offer statement is the foundational requirement. The buyer must be able to determine, from the decision stage itself, precisely what they are purchasing or committing to. The offer statement must be complete — naming the product or service, its scope, and its primary deliverable — and must not require the buyer to retain information from earlier in the path or infer unstated elements.

Visible commitment terms must be present before the primary action is requested. The buyer must know the price, the billing interval if recurring, the commitment duration, and any material obligations before they are asked to accept them. Commitment terms that appear only after the action is initiated — in a checkout flow, in fine print, in post-purchase documentation — constitute commitment ambiguity and will consistently depress completion rates at the final conversion step.

Trust infrastructure calibrated to commitment size must be positioned at or proximate to the decision stage. Trust infrastructure includes evidence elements — testimonials, case studies, credentials, guarantees — that reduce the perceived risk of the commitment. The calibration requirement means that the density and type of trust infrastructure must match the commitment level: a low-risk micro-commitment requires minimal trust infrastructure while a high-risk purchase requires comprehensive evidence positioning.

A single primary action is required at each decision stage. Multiple competing actions at the decision moment — multiple CTAs, navigation elements that pull attention, secondary offers — distribute buyer attention and increase the probability that no action is taken. The path must collapse to a single executable action at the commitment point.

A next-step explanation describes what happens immediately after the primary action is taken. Buyers approaching commitment experience uncertainty about the process that will follow. The next-step explanation resolves this uncertainty before it prevents action, reducing the process ambiguity that is among the most common decision stage failure modes.

Calibration to Commitment Size

Decision stage integrity requirements scale with the size and reversibility of the commitment being requested. This scaling principle is among the most practically consequential aspects of decision stage architecture and among the most frequently misapplied.

A micro-commitment — an email subscription, a free trial initiation, a low-cost entry purchase — carries minimal financial risk and high reversibility. The structural threshold for integrity at this commitment level is correspondingly lower. A complete offer statement, a single primary action, and a minimal next-step explanation are sufficient. Extensive trust infrastructure, detailed commitment term documentation, and comprehensive value delivery timelines are not required — and when present, they can introduce friction that reduces rather than supports conversion at the micro-commitment level.

A high-ticket purchase — a significant financial commitment, an annual subscription, a high-cost service engagement — carries substantial financial risk and reduced reversibility. The structural threshold for integrity at this commitment level is materially higher. All five structural elements must be present and fully developed. Trust infrastructure must be comprehensive and specific to the buyer's likely objections. Commitment terms must be unambiguous and proactively disclosed. The next-step explanation must provide a clear model of the entire post-purchase process, not just the immediate next step.

The structural error that most commonly damages conversion rates is mismatched architecture: applying low-commitment-level architecture to a high-commitment decision stage, or applying high-commitment-level architecture to a micro-commitment request. Both produce failure — the first through structural incompleteness at a stage that requires full architecture, the second through friction and cognitive load at a stage where the barrier should be minimal. Correct calibration requires mapping every decision stage's commitment level and ensuring the architecture matches the structural requirements that commitment level imposes.

Decision Stage Audit Logic

Evaluating decision stage integrity requires a structured audit approach that distinguishes structural gaps from optimization opportunities. The distinction is consequential: structural gaps must be corrected before optimization work proceeds; optimization opportunities can be addressed in sequence after structural completeness is achieved.

The 5-point structural check applies each structural requirement as a binary pass/fail evaluation at each decision-critical position in the path. A decision stage passes the offer statement check if a buyer encountering the stage for the first time, with no prior path context, can determine precisely what they are purchasing. A decision stage passes the commitment terms check if price, duration, and material obligations are visible before the action is requested. A decision stage passes the trust infrastructure check if evidence elements are present and scaled to the commitment level. A decision stage passes the single action check if no competing actions of equal or greater visual weight are present at the commitment moment. A decision stage passes the next-step check if the post-action process is described before the action is requested.

Positions that fail any check have a structural gap. The gap must be characterized — which element is absent, and what is the nature of the absence — and corrected structurally before conversion rate optimization work is initiated at that stage. Positions that pass all five checks have achieved structural completeness and are candidates for optimization work. The Funnelplugs Protocol specifies the sequencing of structural correction and optimization work. The Revenue Leak Diagnostic Engine applies this audit logic interactively across the full commercial path.

Classification

Decision Stage Integrity is the governing structural concept for the Decision Ambiguity failure class within the Plug Ontology. Decision Ambiguity is the failure condition that results when decision stage integrity is absent at a commitment-required position. Decision Stage Integrity is the standard against which that failure is diagnosed and the target state to which the structural correction aspires.

The minimum structural completeness thresholds for decision stages at each commitment level are defined in the Funnelplugs Standard. The Standard governs what constitutes a structurally complete decision stage across commitment types, enabling consistent diagnosis and comparison across commercial paths.
